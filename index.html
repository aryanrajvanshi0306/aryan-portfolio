Deep Learning NLP Finance

Hybrid LSTM-Transformer Stock Volatility Predictor

Traditional volatility models failed during high-sentiment events — the exact moments that matter most. I built a deep learning architecture that combined sequence memory with attention mechanisms, and made it explain itself in the three most common words on a trading desk: "why did it."

0%
next-day volatility accuracy
LSTM Transformer FinBERT PyTorch

The Brief

Build a volatility prediction model that works during sentiment-driven spikes — not just calm market conditions. 10+ years of OHLCV, Google Trends, and Reddit/Twitter sentiment as inputs.

The Hard Parts

  • Severe class imbalance — volatility spikes are rare, but that's exactly when you need to predict them
  • Sentiment signals arrive at different granularities than price data — multi-modal fusion problem
  • Black-box predictions are useless on a trading desk. Interpretability was non-negotiable.

The Result

Focal Loss handles the imbalance. Attention maps surface the top 3 drivers per prediction — sentiment, trend, or price momentum. 78% accuracy. Interpretable by design, not as an afterthought.

How I work.

01

Start with what looks wrong.

I spent two hours building on "clean" data once, then found 7% of records were missing categories. Now I always start by hunting for suspicion, not readiness. The data that looks fine is the data that bites you.

02

If it can't be explained, it can't be trusted.

A black box saying "volatility high" is useless on a trading desk. I focus on attention maps and feature weights so the people using the output can understand — and challenge — what it's saying. Especially the challenging part.

03

Optimise for decisions, not dashboards.

The last question I ask is: "What will someone do differently tomorrow because of this?" If I can't answer, I haven't finished. A beautiful chart that changes nothing is decoration, not analysis.

04

The best insight leads to action.

Catching a battery thermal anomaly mid-cycle is the difference between an interesting chart and a useful system. Data that doesn't change behaviour is noise. I build for the signal. Always the signal.
